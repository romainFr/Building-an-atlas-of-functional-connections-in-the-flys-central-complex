Since no single characteristic of the responses could adequately capture their variety, variability and complexity,  we chose to summarize the transients obtained with a battery of statistics reflecting response amplitude, shape, reliability and stimulus sensitivity  (see Figure \ref{489066}C, Supplementary Figure \ref{695432}, and Materials and Methods for a full description). Responses of non-overlapping pairs were then used to form the null-hypothesis distributions of two of the statistics capturing response amplitude and reliability (see Figure \ref{489066}D). For every data point, the Mahalanobis distance to the mean of the null distribution was computed and pairs were considered as connected if this distance fell significantly outside of the distance distribution of the null sample (for details see Materials and Methods).
The resulting connectivity matrix is shown in Figure \ref{384795}. It is worth noting two things at this stage. First, non-overlapping pairs usually showed no fluctuations upon stimulation, and when they did, those were small and unreliable (see Figure \ref{489066}Di), likely reflecting indirect effects. This suggests that the effects observed with our stimulation protocol were likely the result of direct synaptic connections. Secondly, and not surprisingly, the same-cell-type-stimulation controls were always detected as responding (see Figure \ref{489066}Dii).
