The central complex consists of three main neuropiles -- the protocerebral bridge (PB), the ellipsoid body (EB, Central Body Lower in other insects) and the fan-shaped body (FB, Central Body Upper in other insects) — and at least three accessory neuropiles — the noduli (NO), gall (GA) and lateral accessory lobe (LAL) (Figure \ref{288889}A and \citealt{Hanesch1989,Wolff2015,Lin2013}). The most striking neural elements of the central complex are the columnar neurons, which innervate one of the eighteen glomeruli of the PB, one vertical section of either the FB or EB, and one accessory neuropile -- the column being constituted by the PB glomeruli and FB/EB section. A total of 12 different columnar cell types have been described, with stereotypical correspondences between the PB glomerulus the and EB/FB section. In addition to these "principal cells", there are a number of neurons innervating multiple columns of one neuropile . This neurons often within subdivisions orthogonal to the columns, and sometimes also, in addition, projecting to neuropiles outside the central complex . This set of neurons includes the ring neurons, which innervate the circumference of the EB and an accessory neuropile, and a collection of inputs and interneurons with processes in the FB and PB. From this light level anatomy and putative synaptic polarity, one can derive a hypothetical general information flow diagram of the central complex (Figure \ref{288889}) :
  • Ring neurons provide input to the EB columnar neurons. 
  • Recurrent connections between EB columnar neurons form and sustain a ring attractor for heading direction
  • Information is transferred from the EB columnar system to the FB columnar system via the PB (interestingly, only one columnar neuron type displays presynaptic terminals in the PB, the E-PGs)
  • FB columnar neurons also receive inputs in the FB
  • All columnar neurons but the E-PGs also receive inputs in the PB
  • Interneurons in the PB and FB further interconnect the columns
  • All accessory structures are potential outputs 
We show that this overall flow of information is generally supported functionally for the parts we have tested so far, but with a few potentially important differences : the connectivity in the PB is very sparse, rendering the function of PB interneurons possibly critical; accessory structures are usually input rather than output areas; and, consequently, output channels of the CX are scarce.

Results

Cell types considered

Through visual inspection of the Janelia Gal4-driver collection \cite{Jenett_2012}, we picked driver lines for central complex cell types based on the strength of expression and the sparseness of the pattern in the central complex. At this stage, the 37 drivers (for 24 cell types) included label the main columnar neuron types and PB interneurons  \cite{wolff_neuroarchitecture_2015}, three types of ring neurons,  a LAL-FB neuron and neurons innervating accessory structures, namely a LAL interneuron and three types of LAL-Noduli neurons (ref Tanya's new paper). Neuron types are schematized in Figure \ref{831433}A and Supplementary Figure \ref{147797}. The drivers used and the nomenclature are listed in Table \ref{786393}. Throughout this paper we follow the naming convention set out in \cite{wolff_neuroarchitecture_2015} for full names, and abbreviated following the logic described in \cite{Kakaria2017} and used in  \cite{Green_2017} and \cite{Turner_Evans_2017}. At the time of writing this manuscript, the dataset explores inputs to the EB system, connections between EB columnar neurons, connections in the PB as well as potential inputs and outputs in the LAL, Gall and noduli. Connectivity of different cell types within the FB have not been explored.

A functional connectivity screen

For any given pair of neurons, the overlap between pre- and post-synaptic looking regions was assessed based on publicly available expression patterns \cite{Jenett2012,Tirian_2017} digitally aligned on a common reference brain (as described in \citealt{Aso_2014}). For each combination selected, one or several genotypes were prepared to express Cs-Chrimson and GCaMP6-m in the potential pre and post-synaptic partners, respectively (see Materials and Methods). Brains and VNCs were dissected out and tested using a standardized protocol (see Figure \ref{831433}D, and Materials and Methods). The light stimulation intensities were kept at low levels (50uW/mm2, delivered in trains of 2 ms pulses) to reduce the chances of observing indirect connections, while at the same time allowing us to see near-saturating responses in strongly connected pairs (see Supplementary Figure \ref{723169}). Whenever large responses were observed (see Materials and Methods), pharmacology was used to check that observed transients were synaptically mediated, and to narrow down the neurotransmitter involved.
